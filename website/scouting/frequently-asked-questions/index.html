What is Cub Scouts about?

In February of 1910, the Boy Scouts of America was founded in Washington, DC. Nine months later, two separate groups met to establish Boy Scout councils in Minnesota: one in Minneapolis and one in St. Paul. Hennepin Council was organized on October 15, 1910. Former President Theodore Roosevelt spoke at the Council’s first general meeting in 1911. Over the next ninety years, both councils expanded.
On July 1, 2005, the Viking and Indianhead Councils merged to form today’s Northern Star Council, one of the largest in the country. The council includes a band of communities reaching from the North Dakota border on the west to the communities of Ellsworth and Roberts, Wisconsin, on the east. The Council’s headquarters is in St. Paul, Minnesota, with a service center in Golden Valley, and Scout Shops in the northern and southern suburbs
Today, Cub Scouts members experience a wide range of social activities and award requirements that develop their self-responsibility, confidence, and service ethic. Neighborhood and family centered, Cub Scouts meet in small groups (dens) in homes or other community locations led by volunteer den leaders, and monthly in pack meetings with families and all other dens. They work on award requirements (advancements) at meetings and with their families, as well as take part in pack/den field trips and special Cub camps.

The Purpose of Cub Scouts

  • Helping build character and encouraging spiritual growth
  • Developing habits and attitudes of good citizenship
  • Encouraging good sportsmanship and pride in growing strong in mind and body
  • Improving understanding within the family
  • Strengthening the ability to get along with other boys and respect other people
  • Fostering a sense of personal achievement by developing new interests and skills
  • Showing how to be helpful and to do one’s best
  • Preparing them to become Boy Scouts

Cub Scout Promise, Motto & Colors

Promise: I promise to do my best, to do my duty to God and my country, to help other people, and to obey the Law of the Pack.
Motto: Do your best.
Colors: The Cub Scout colors are blue and gold. They have special meaning, which will help boys see beyond the fun of Cub Scouting to its ultimate goals. The blue stands for truth and spirituality, steadfast loyalty, and the sky above. The gold stands for warm sunlight, good cheer and happiness.

What do Cub Scouts do?

Advancement

Cub Scout advancement begins with the Bobcat Badge. This is then followed by the age appropriate advancement. First graders work towards their Tiger Cub Badge. Second graders are on the Wolf trail. Third grade Cubs work towards the Bear badge.
Fourth and Fifth grade boys are working towards their Webelos badge, and ultimately, they may earn the Arrow of Light, Cub Scouting’s highest award.

How old are Cub Scouts?

Cub Scouts are in grades K-5.

What is the time commitment?

Den meetings and activities are typically once a month and involve boys of similar age groups.
Pack meetings are once a month for 90 minutes and involve all boys.
Special activities such as camping or service projects are optional and will have specific time commitments to each activity.

What does it cost?

Cub Scout dues are $60 per year.
Activity costs will be specific to each optional activity but are usually at least partially paid by the pack from fund raising proceeds.

Is there fund raising?

Yes, the boys sell and deliver holiday wreaths in the late fall. They sell very easily and boys can earn prizes for set targets reached.

Who are the leaders and how are they selected and trained?

All adult leaders are volunteers. All adult leaders complete training courses organized by the BSA for their specific positions in the Pack. ALL ADULT volunteers are REQUIRED to complete Youth Protection Training.

A Cub Scout pack committee is essentially the board of directors for a Cub Scout pack. This committee is a group of at least three people who assist with supervision and management of the unit. They provide resources and support to the Cubmaster and Den Leaders so they can provide the program in accordance with Boy Scout policies and regulations. Pack 342’s Leadership Committee includes:

  • Cubmaster
  • Den Leaders
  • Pack Committee Chairperson
  • Pack Committee Members
  • Recruiting
  • Camping
  • Finance
  • Service Projects
  • Fund Raising
  • Training Coordinator
  • Community Service Coordinator
  • Friends of Scouting Event Coordinator
  • Camping Chair

How do we go from Cub Scouts to Boy Scouts?

When cubs have completed Webelos II they will “crossover” into Boy Scouts. The Boy Scouts support our Cub programs throughout the year. By the time Cubs are old enough to join Boy Scouts they will already have friends in the troop that have crossed over before them.

What can I do as a parent to support my Cub Scout?

Cub Scouts is a parent / child program. You will show support through your participation with your son, or sons, in the program. From time to time, the Pack Committee will ask for volunteers to help with specific events and all help is greatly appreciated! If anyone has additional time or talents that they wish to share by becoming a Pack Committee Member, there is always a need. Please reach out to one of the Committee Members for more information.
